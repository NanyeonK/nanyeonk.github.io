What Is Tokenization?

Last updated: March 29, 2026 Tokenization
Contents
  • Definition
  • How It Works
  • Why It Matters for Real Estate
  • Key Distinctions
  • Current State

Definition

Tokenization is the process of representing ownership rights in a real-world asset as a digital token on a blockchain. When applied to securities, the resulting tokens are called security tokens, and the process of offering them to investors is a security token offering (STO).

Unlike utility tokens or cryptocurrencies, security tokens are subject to securities regulations. They represent actual financial claims: equity, debt, revenue shares, or fractional ownership in an underlying asset. The token itself is not the asset. It is a digital record of ownership that references an off-chain legal structure.

How It Works

The typical process involves several layers:

Legal structure. An issuer creates a legal entity (often an SPV or LLC) that holds the real-world asset. The entity’s ownership interest is divided into units that correspond to tokens.

Smart contract. A smart contract on a blockchain defines the token’s rules: total supply, transfer restrictions, compliance checks, and distribution of any cash flows. Common standards include ERC-1400 and ERC-3643 on Ethereum.

Issuance platform. The issuer works with a tokenization platform that handles the technical issuance, KYC/AML verification of investors, and often provides a primary market for the initial sale.

Secondary trading. After issuance, tokens may trade on regulated alternative trading systems (ATS) or other compliant venues, providing liquidity that direct real estate ownership typically lacks.

Why It Matters for Real Estate

Real estate is among the most frequently discussed asset classes for tokenization, for good reason. The asset class has several properties that make it a natural candidate:

  • High minimum investment. Direct real estate ownership requires substantial capital. Tokenization allows fractional ownership with lower minimums.
  • Illiquidity. Real estate transactions are slow and costly. Tokens can trade on secondary markets with faster settlement.
  • Opacity. Real estate markets have limited price transparency. On-chain records provide a verifiable audit trail.
  • Global demand. Cross-border real estate investment faces significant friction. Tokenization can streamline access for international investors through programmable compliance.

Key Distinctions

It is important to distinguish tokenization from related concepts:

  • Tokenization vs. REITs. REITs pool multiple properties and trade on stock exchanges. Tokens can represent a single property and trade on specialized venues. See REIT vs STO for a detailed comparison.
  • Tokenization vs. crowdfunding. Real estate crowdfunding platforms aggregate small investments but typically do not offer secondary market liquidity or on-chain settlement.
  • Security tokens vs. utility tokens. Security tokens represent financial claims and are regulated as securities. Utility tokens provide access to a service and are generally not considered securities (though the boundary is contested).

Current State

As of early 2026, the tokenized real estate market remains small relative to traditional real estate investment but has grown significantly. Several regulated platforms now offer primary issuance and secondary trading of real estate tokens in the United States, European Union, and Asia-Pacific jurisdictions. The total market capitalization of tokenized real estate securities is estimated in the low billions of USD, though precise figures depend on how one defines the category.
