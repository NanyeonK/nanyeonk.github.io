REIT vs STO

Last updated: March 29, 2026 Real Estate Tokenization
Contents

  • Structure
  • Regulation

Overview

Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) and Security Token Offerings (STOs) both provide investors with exposure to real estate, but they differ substantially in structure, regulation, liquidity mechanisms, and investor experience. This page compares the two along dimensions that matter for both practitioners and researchers.

Structure

REITs are pooled investment vehicles. A REIT holds a portfolio of properties (or real estate debt) and issues shares that trade on public exchanges or are offered privately. REITs must satisfy specific regulatory requirements: in the US, they must distribute at least 90% of taxable income as dividends, hold at least 75% of assets in real estate, and have a minimum number of shareholders.

STOs are more flexible in structure. A single property can be tokenized through an SPV, or a portfolio can be represented as a single token series. There are no mandatory distribution ratios (beyond what the offering documents specify), and the compliance rules are embedded in the smart contract rather than in entity-level regulation.

Regulation

Dimension REIT STO
Registration SEC-registered (public) or exempt (private) Typically Reg D, Reg S, or Reg A+ exempt
Ongoing reporting 10-K, 10-Q filings for public REITs Varies by exemption; generally lighter
Investor eligibility Open to all (public) or accredited (private) Typically accredited investors (Reg D)
Distribution rules 90% income distribution required Set by offering terms

Liquidity

Public REITs trade on major stock exchanges with deep liquidity, tight spreads, and continuous price discovery. This liquidity comes at a cost: REIT prices are heavily correlated with equity markets, which can reduce the diversification benefit of real estate exposure.

STO secondary markets are thinner. Tokens trade on regulated alternative trading systems with lower volume and wider spreads. However, because each token can represent a specific property, the price may more closely track the underlying real estate value rather than equity market sentiment. This is an empirical question that remains open.

Cost Structure

REITs charge management fees (typically 0.5%–1.5% of NAV) and often have acquisition and disposition fees. The REIT structure also introduces operational overhead for regulatory compliance.

STOs incur issuance costs (legal structuring, platform fees, smart contract auditing) that can be substantial for small offerings. However, ongoing management costs may be lower because smart contracts automate some compliance and distribution functions. The breakeven depends on the size and duration of the offering.

Investor Experience

REIT investors receive a familiar security: a share that can be bought and sold through a standard brokerage account. Dividends arrive automatically. Tax reporting follows established procedures (though REIT taxation has its own complexities).

STO investors must typically use a specialized platform, maintain a digital wallet (or use a custodial service), and navigate a less mature investor infrastructure. The friction is higher but has been decreasing as platforms improve their interfaces and custody solutions mature.

Research Implications

The REIT-vs-STO comparison raises several research questions:

  1. Price discovery. Do STO prices reflect property fundamentals more accurately than REIT prices, given the latter’s equity market correlation?
  2. Liquidity premium. What discount do investors require for the lower liquidity of STO secondary markets relative to public REITs?
  3. Diversification. Does the property-level granularity of STOs provide diversification benefits that REIT portfolios cannot?
  4. Cost efficiency. At what scale does an STO structure become more cost-efficient than a REIT structure for the issuer?

These questions have not yet been answered rigorously in the academic literature, partly because the STO market is too young to produce the long time series that empirical real estate finance typically requires.
