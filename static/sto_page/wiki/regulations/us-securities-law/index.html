The Howey Test

The foundational question in US securities law is whether a given instrument is a “security” under the Securities Act of 1933. The Supreme Court’s 1946 decision in SEC v. W.J. Howey Co. established the test: an instrument is a security if it involves (1) an investment of money, (2) in a common enterprise, (3) with an expectation of profit, (4) derived from the efforts of others.

Real estate security tokens almost always satisfy this test. Investors put money into a token representing ownership in an SPV that holds property. The expectation is that rental income or property appreciation will generate returns. And the management of the property is handled by a third party, not the token holders. The SEC has consistently treated such arrangements as securities.

Registration and Exemptions

Securities must be registered with the SEC or qualify for an exemption from registration. Full registration (an S-1 filing) is expensive and time-consuming, so most real estate STOs rely on exemptions:

Regulation D (Rule 506(b) and 506(c)). The most common path. Rule 506(b) allows raising unlimited capital from accredited investors and up to 35 sophisticated but non-accredited investors, with no general solicitation. Rule 506(c) allows general solicitation but restricts sales to verified accredited investors.

Regulation S. Permits offerings to non-US investors without SEC registration, provided the offering is made outside the United States and includes restrictions on flowback into US markets.

Regulation A+ (Tier 2). Allows raising up to $75 million from both accredited and non-accredited investors, with simplified reporting. This path provides broader investor access but requires SEC qualification of an offering circular.

Regulation CF. Crowdfunding exemption allowing up to $5 million from any investor, but the low cap limits its usefulness for real estate.

Transfer Restrictions

A critical aspect of securities law for tokenized assets is the restriction on resale. Securities issued under Reg D are “restricted securities” that cannot be freely resold. Investors must hold for at least 12 months (Rule 144) before reselling, and even then only to other qualified buyers or through a registered broker-dealer.

Smart contracts can enforce these restrictions programmatically. Token standards like ERC-1400 and ERC-3643 include built-in transfer validation that checks whether a proposed transfer complies with applicable holding periods, investor qualification, and jurisdictional restrictions.

Broker-Dealer and ATS Requirements

Platforms that facilitate secondary trading of security tokens must be registered as broker-dealers and operate a regulated Alternative Trading System (ATS). The SEC has been clear that token trading venues handling securities must comply with the same rules as traditional securities exchanges, regardless of the underlying technology.

Several platforms now hold ATS licenses and facilitate secondary trading of real estate tokens within the regulatory framework. The compliance infrastructure is functional but adds cost and complexity relative to unregulated token markets.

Ongoing Developments

The regulatory landscape continues to evolve. The SEC has issued guidance on digital asset securities, and several enforcement actions have clarified the boundaries. Key areas of ongoing development include:

  • Clarity on the treatment of tokenized fund interests versus direct property tokens
  • Standards for digital asset custody by registered investment advisers
  • Cross-border regulatory coordination for multi-jurisdictional offerings
  • Potential rulemaking on tokenized securities specifically (as distinct from general digital asset guidance)

Practitioners should monitor SEC releases and no-action letters for the latest developments.
