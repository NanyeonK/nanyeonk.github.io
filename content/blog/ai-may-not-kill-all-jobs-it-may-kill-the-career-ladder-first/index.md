- Report: [THE 2028 GLOBAL INTELLIGENCE CRISIS](https://www.citriniresearch.com/p/2028gic)

Citrini Research’s *The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis* is an intentionally frightening report. But that is also why it is worth reading. The tone is dramatic, but the mechanism is not ridiculous.

I think the view that this report helped push the Nasdaq lower on the day and the day after is reasonably plausible. At the time, markets were already nervous about weak SaaS earnings, AI substitution, and the possibility that productivity gains would not translate into broad income gains. This report did not create that fear. It organized it. It gave existing anxiety a cleaner story and pushed it further.

What makes the report interesting is not the shallow claim that AI will destroy jobs. The more serious point is that large parts of white-collar work may be structurally replaceable for the first time.

We have seen machines replace physical labor before. That is old history. What feels different now is that routine cognitive labor is also becoming automatable at scale: bookkeeping, document work, repetitive research, lightweight design, and all the small judgment tasks that used to require a junior employee sitting in front of a screen all day.

That matters because advanced economies are not built around farms anymore, and many of them are not psychologically built around factories either. They are built around offices. They are built around the expectation that educated people move into white-collar work, spend years climbing, and eventually turn experience into income.

That is also why I am skeptical of simplistic “bring manufacturing back” politics. Even if production returns, that does not mean workers will. A country can want more factories without its population wanting factory jobs.

For more on that, see: [Will Americans really go back to manufacturing?](/blog/will-americans-really-go-back-to-manufacturing)

The first jobs likely to be hit are not the most creative ones. They are the jobs that are closer to mental manual labor: simple accounting tasks, repetitive reporting, basic cover design, template-based visual work, low-level administrative analysis. In the past, these were often dismissed as easy jobs. That misses the point.

They were not important because they were glamorous. They were important because they were training grounds.

A large part of junior labor existed so firms could give young workers repetitive, low-stakes work while they learned judgment. That was how people became useful. That was how they became senior. If AI removes that layer, it does not just cut labor costs. It removes part of the ladder.

That is the part I think many people still understate. Firms may increasingly want people who can perform at a senior level from day one. In the old system, companies hired juniors, let them do repetitive work, and trained them over time. In the new system, if AI can do much of that repetitive work, the incentive to hire broadly and train patiently gets weaker.

The result is a harsher labor market. Firms may hire fewer people, and among those they hire, they may increasingly prefer workers who can contribute immediately. Many people will not be excluded because they are unintelligent. They will be excluded because the path that used to turn them into valuable workers is shrinking.

If that process continues, white-collar work may start to require something closer to graduate-school timelines. I do not mean everyone will literally need a master’s degree or a PhD. I mean more people may spend years in low-income or no-income states while they accumulate credentials, signal ability, and delay real labor market entry.

That creates a strange social outcome: adulthood by age, but not by income. In that world, many people may be legally adult for a decade before they become economically independent. East Asia already looks uncomfortably close to that reality.

For more on that, see: [Adults by age, not by income](/blog/adults-by-age-not-by-income)

So my reaction to this report is simple. It is scary, but it is not crazy.

Its main point is sharper than many of the usual AI takes. Yes, AI can raise productivity. But if those gains do not return to households as income, the wider economy can still weaken. Firms can produce more with fewer workers. Those workers then spend less. Lower spending weakens the consumer economy. Productivity rises while demand erodes. That tension is the real point of the report.

I do not think this scenario will unfold exactly as written. That is not the standard that matters. The report is useful because it highlights a real possibility: AI can be excellent for firms and still destabilizing for society.

The most immediate risk may not be that AI replaces all human labor. It may be that firms stop building humans at scale.

If that happens, the first thing AI kills may not be jobs themselves. It may be the career ladder that used to make those jobs possible.
