A few weeks ago, I asked an AI agent to find the cheapest option for something I needed. In seconds, it compared prices, shipping, and return policies and gave me a clean answer. No tab chaos, no coupon rabbit hole, no “wait, did I miss a better deal?” feeling.

That tiny moment made me rethink a bigger thing. A lot of middleman business still exists because normal people pay a time-and-stress tax when they search, compare, and verify.

To be fair, intermediaries are not useless. They reduce confusion. They know where to look, whom to call, and how to turn scattered information into a decision. But economically, much of that is still an information gap business. If the gap gets smaller, the margin usually gets smaller too.

I think AI agents matter here in a deeper way than “nice UX.” A good agent does not just answer once. It keeps scanning, applies your rules, and acts fast without getting tired. Work that used to take hours of coordination can start looking like a background task.

And this is only half the story. The other half is trust. Many middle layers survive because counterparties do not fully trust each other, so they pay third parties to verify ownership, sequence payments, and guarantee settlement.

Tokenization starts to pressure that layer. If ownership and transfer conditions are transparent and executable, some verification steps become cheaper and some become unnecessary. Not everything disappears, but the list of “must-have intermediaries” gets shorter.

What feels different now is the combination. Agents reduce information asymmetry. Tokenization reduces trust asymmetry. Together, they compress the full path from discovery to settlement.

So no, I do not think every intermediary is doomed. High-context work still has strong value: judgment under ambiguity, real expertise, accountability when things break. But low-context, mechanical intermediation is going to be under pressure.

The internet lowered information scarcity. Agents plus tokenization are lowering transaction scarcity. The everyday arbitrage we barely noticed is still around, but it already feels less permanent than it did even a year ago.
